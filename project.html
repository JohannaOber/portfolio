Bloom — Shopping Redesign

End-to-end redesign of a retail app's checkout flow, reducing drop-off through progressive disclosure and inline error handling.

My Role

Lead UX Designer

Client

Bloom GmbH

Duration

3 months · 2024

Tasks
UX Research Journey Mapping Prototyping User Testing Handoff
Challenge

The checkout was losing customers at every step.

Bloom's existing checkout flow had a high drop-off rate. Error messages appeared only after submission, form fields were unclear, and the overall structure buried the most critical actions below the fold. Users were confused and frustrated — and they left.

Process

Discovery & Research

Conducted moderated usability sessions with existing customers, analysed funnel drop-off data, and mapped pain points across the current flow to build a shared understanding of where and why users were leaving.

Define & Structure

Synthesised findings into a revised information architecture and progressive disclosure model — reducing the checkout from 6 screens to 3 focused steps with clearer hierarchy and less cognitive load.

Design & Prototype

Designed high-fidelity screens in Figma with inline validation, clear progress indicators, and a persistent order summary. Built a linked prototype for stakeholder alignment and user testing.

Test & Iterate

Two rounds of unmoderated testing with participants via Maze. Iterated on field grouping, CTA placement, and error copy based on task completion rates and qualitative feedback.

Solution

A checkout that earns trust at every step.

The redesigned flow surfaces the most important information at the right moment. Inline validation prevents dead ends, a persistent summary keeps context visible, and the condensed 3-step structure reduces cognitive load without sacrificing flexibility.

Impact

A measurably smoother experience for every user.

The new checkout launched to positive feedback from both users and the client team. Drop-off decreased significantly, and user testing sessions showed a marked improvement in confidence and task completion. The design has since been adopted as a reference pattern within the product.
